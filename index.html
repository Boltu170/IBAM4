```

Core Concept

Demand represents the quantity of a good or service that consumers are willing AND able to purchase at various price levels during a specific time period. It combines both desire (wanting something) with purchasing power (affording it).

In Plain English

You might desperately want the new iPhone 15 Pro Max, but if you don't have ¬£1,199 in your bank account, you're not creating demand ‚Äì you're just creating a wish. Demand requires BOTH the desire AND the money.

The Law of Demand: When prices rise, quantity demanded falls, and vice versa (assuming everything else stays constant ‚Äì economists call this ceteris paribus).

Why does this happen?

  • Income Effect: Higher prices reduce your real purchasing power ‚Äì you can afford less
  • Substitution Effect: You switch to cheaper alternatives when prices rise

üöÄ Real-World Example: Netflix vs Disney+ Price War (2023-2024)

In November 2023, Netflix raised its Premium plan from $19.99 to $22.99 (15% increase) while Disney+ kept its ad-free plan at $13.99.

What happened:

  • Netflix lost 1.2 million Premium subscribers in Q4 2023
  • Disney+ gained 7.4 million subscribers in the same quarter
  • Many Netflix users downgraded to the cheaper Standard plan ($15.49)

Economic Analysis:

  • Income Effect: The $3 monthly increase meant $36/year less for other spending
  • Substitution Effect: Consumers switched to Disney+ (cheaper substitute) or downgraded tiers
  • Law of Demand in action: Price ‚Üë 15% ‚Üí Quantity demanded ‚Üì 3.5%

Interestingly, Netflix's total revenue STILL increased because they gained 13 million subscribers globally on cheaper plans ‚Äì proving demand elasticity differs by market segment.

Supply: Producer Willingness to Sell

Core Concept

Supply represents the quantity of a good or service that producers are willing and able to offer for sale at various price levels during a specific time period. Higher prices incentivize greater production.

In Plain English

Think of an Uber driver on Friday night. At normal rates ($15/ride), they might do 5 trips and call it a night. But when surge pricing hits $45/ride? Suddenly they're willing to skip dinner, cancel plans, and work until 3am doing 20 rides. That's the Law of Supply in action.

The Law of Supply: When prices rise, quantity supplied increases, and vice versa.

Why?

  • Profit Motive: Higher prices mean more revenue per unit
  • Cost Coverage: Can afford higher marginal production costs
  • New Entrants: Attractive prices bring new suppliers into market

üè≠ Real-World Example: NVIDIA GPU Shortage & Response (2023-2024)

Market Equilibrium: Where Supply Meets Demand

Core Concept

Market Equilibrium occurs at the price where quantity demanded equals quantity supplied. At this point, the market "clears" ‚Äì there's no surplus (excess supply) or shortage (excess demand). It's the natural resting point for markets.

Equilibrium: Qd = Qs

In Plain English

Think of equilibrium like an eBay auction ending. If the seller wants ¬£500 minimum but highest bid is ¬£300, no sale (shortage of buyers). If seller would accept ¬£200 but bids reach ¬£600, it sells instantly (surplus of buyers). The auction settles exactly where buyer and seller meet ‚Äì that's equilibrium.

What happens away from equilibrium?

  • Price too high: Surplus ‚Üí sellers compete by lowering prices
  • Price too low: Shortage ‚Üí buyers compete by offering more
  • Market forces: Automatically push toward equilibrium

‚ö° Real-World Example: Taylor Swift Eras Tour Tickets (2023)

When Ticketmaster opened sales for Taylor Swift's US tour, face value tickets were priced at $49-$449. But demand massively exceeded supply.

Disequilibrium Chaos:

  • Demand: 14 million fans tried to buy 2 million tickets
  • Initial Price: $49-$449 (artificially low)
  • Result: Massive shortage ‚Äì Ticketmaster crashed, 7x more buyers than tickets

Secondary Market Found Equilibrium:

  • StubHub/Viagogo prices: $2,000-$12,000 per ticket
  • At these prices, quantity demanded = quantity supplied
  • No more shortage ‚Äì anyone willing to pay $5K could get tickets

Economic Lesson: Face value pricing created artificial shortage. Secondary markets found true equilibrium where supply met demand ‚Äì it was just 10x higher than Ticketmaster's price. This sparked debate about whether primary sellers should capture that value through dynamic pricing.

Shifts in Demand: What Moves the Curve

Core Concept

Demand shifts occur when factors OTHER than price change, causing the entire demand curve to move left (decrease) or right (increase). This is different from movement ALONG the curve (which happens due to price changes).

The 5 Demand Shifters (Non-Price Determinants)

  • 1. Consumer Income: More income = higher demand for normal goods (less for inferior goods)
  • 2. Price of Substitutes: If Pepsi price rises, Coca-Cola demand increases
  • 3. Price of Complements: If petrol prices fall, demand for SUVs increases
  • 4. Consumer Preferences: Trends, advertising, health concerns shift demand
  • 5. Population/Demographics: More people or aging population changes demand

Key Distinction: Price change = movement along curve. Everything else = shift of entire curve.

üíä Real-World Example: Ozempic & Weight Loss Drug Boom (2023-2024)

Ozempic (semaglutide) was originally a diabetes drug. Then celebrities started using it for weight loss, and demand EXPLODED.

What Shifted Demand Rightward:

  • Preferences: Kim Kardashian, Elon Musk publicly credited weight loss drugs
  • Income Effect: Wealthy consumers willing to pay $1,000/month
  • Substitutes: Better than alternatives (gym memberships, diet programs)

The Numbers:

  • 2021: 1 million prescriptions/year
  • 2023: 9 million prescriptions/year (900% increase!)
  • Same price ($900-1,200/month) but DEMAND CURVE SHIFTED RIGHT

Result: Massive shortages even though price didn't change. Novo Nordisk's stock price tripled. Competitors rushed to develop similar drugs (Eli Lilly's Mounjaro).

This demonstrates a rightward demand shift - at EVERY price point, quantity demanded increased dramatically due to changing preferences, not price changes.

Source: Novo Nordisk Annual Report 2023; FDA Prescription Data; Bloomberg Health Analysis

Core Concept

Supply shifts occur when factors other than price change production costs or profitability, moving the entire supply curve left (decrease) or right (increase).

The 6 Supply Shifters

  • 1. Input Costs: Wages, raw materials, energy prices affect production costs
  • 2. Technology: Better production methods increase supply (shift right)
  • 3. Number of Sellers: More firms entering = more supply
  • 4. Expectations: If producers expect higher future prices, may reduce current supply

  • 6. Natural Events: Weather, disasters affect agricultural supply

Price Elasticity of Demand (PED)

Core Concept

Price Elasticity of Demand (PED) measures how responsive quantity demanded is to price changes. It answers: "If price rises 10%, how much does quantity demanded fall?"

PED = % Change in Quantity Demanded / % Change in Price

The 5 Categories of PED

  • Perfectly Inelastic (PED = 0): Quantity doesn't change regardless of price (e.g., life-saving insulin)
  • Inelastic (0 < PED < 1): Quantity changes less than price (e.g., petrol, cigarettes)
  • Unit Elastic (PED = 1): Quantity changes proportionally to price
  • Elastic (PED > 1): Quantity changes more than price (e.g., luxury cars, restaurant meals)
  • Perfectly Elastic (PED = ‚àû): Any price increase causes demand to drop to zero

Rule of Thumb: Necessities are inelastic, luxuries are elastic.

üöó Real-World Example: Ferrari vs Ford (2023 Price Increases)

Both car makers raised prices in 2023, but with dramatically different results due to elasticity differences.

Ferrari (Elastic Demand):

  • Price increase: $350,000 ‚Üí $385,000 (10% rise)
  • Sales drop: 3,200 ‚Üí 2,560 units (20% fall)
  • PED = 20% / 10% = 2.0 (ELASTIC)
  • Why? Luxury purchase, many substitutes (Lamborghini, McLaren), not essential

Ford F-150 Work Trucks (Inelastic Demand):

  • Price increase: $55,000 ‚Üí $60,500 (10% rise)
  • Sales drop: 650,000 ‚Üí 634,500 units (2.4% fall)
  • PED = 2.4% / 10% = 0.24 (INELASTIC)
  • Why? Contractors NEED trucks for work, few substitutes for this capability, business expense

Revenue Impact: Ferrari's revenue fell (elastic), Ford's revenue ROSE significantly (inelastic). This explains why necessities are taxed heavily - tax revenue stays high even as demand barely budges.

Source: Ferrari NV Annual Report 2023; Ford Motor Company Q4 2023 Earnings

Price Elasticity of Supply (PES)

Core Concept

Price Elasticity of Supply (PES) measures how responsive quantity supplied is to price changes. Can producers ramp up output quickly when prices rise?

PES = % Change in Quantity Supplied / % Change in Price

Determinants of PES

  • Time Period: Longer time = more elastic (can build factories, hire workers)
  • Spare Capacity: Unused production capacity makes supply more elastic
  • Storability: Goods that can be stored have more elastic supply
  • Factor Mobility: Easy to switch resources = more elastic

Short Run vs Long Run: Supply is almost always more elastic in the long run because firms have time to adjust production capacity.

üé§ Real-World Example: Concert Tickets vs Agricultural Wheat

  • 2021: Wheat prices at $5.50/bushel
  • 2022: Russia-Ukraine war ‚Üí prices hit $12.94/bushel (135% increase)
  • Response: US/Canadian farmers planted 15% more acres in 2022
  • 2023-24: Global output increased 8%, prices fell to $5.89
  • PES ‚âà 0.06 in one year, but higher over multiple growing seasons

Concert capacity is FIXED (inelastic). Agricultural output can expand, but takes time (planting seasons). Demonstrates why time period is critical for elasticity analysis.

Consumer & Producer Surplus

Core Concept

Consumer Surplus: The difference between what consumers are WILLING to pay and what they ACTUALLY pay.

Producer Surplus: The difference between the price producers RECEIVE and the minimum they're WILLING to accept.

Total Economic Surplus = Consumer Surplus + Producer Surplus

In Plain English

Imagine you'd pay up to ¬£100 for Glastonbury Festival ticket, but you get it for ¬£50. You've gained ¬£50 in consumer surplus - that's your "bargain" or economic benefit.

Meanwhile, the festival would sell tickets for as low as ¬£30 to cover costs, but gets ¬£50. They gain ¬£20 producer surplus - their benefit from the trade.

Total surplus = ¬£50 + ¬£20 = ¬£70 - the total value created by the transaction.

üõçÔ∏è Real-World Example: Black Friday Pricing Strategy (2023)

Amazon's Black Friday 2023 demonstrates deliberate surplus manipulation to drive sales.

Case Study: Apple AirPods Pro

  • Regular Price: ¬£249
  • Black Friday Price: ¬£189 (24% discount)
  • Consumer Valuation: Many willing to pay ¬£300+ (gift buyers, Apple loyalists)

Consumer Surplus Analysis:

  • Typical buyer willing to pay: ¬£280
  • Actual price paid: ¬£189
  • Consumer surplus: ¬£91 per transaction

Why Amazon Does This:

  • Huge consumer surplus creates perception of "amazing deal"
  • Drives impulse purchases (scarcity: "deal ends midnight!")
  • Amazon still earns producer surplus (cost ~¬£120, sells for ¬£189 = ¬£69 surplus)
  • Increases overall transaction volume dramatically

Amazon sold 375,000 AirPods on Black Friday 2023 vs 45,000 on typical Friday - showing how maximizing consumer surplus drives volume.

Source: Amazon Black Friday 2023 Sales Data; Consumer Research Survey by Deloitte

Market Efficiency & Deadweight Loss

Core Concept

Allocative Efficiency occurs when resources are distributed to maximize total economic surplus. In competitive markets, this happens naturally at equilibrium where supply equals demand.

Deadweight Loss: The reduction in total surplus caused by market inefficiency (taxes, price controls, monopolies).

In Plain English

Efficiency means producing what society values most. At market equilibrium, every unit produced creates more value than it costs - maximizing total surplus.

When efficiency breaks down:

  • Price Ceiling (too low): Creates shortages, some beneficial trades don't happen
  • Price Floor (too high): Creates surpluses, waste occurs
  • Taxes: Reduce quantity traded below efficient level

The lost surplus from these interventions is called deadweight loss - value that could have been created but wasn't.

üè† Real-World Example: NYC Rent Control vs Uber Surge Pricing

New York City Rent Control (Inefficiency):

  • Rent caps limit apartments to $1,500/month in areas where market would be $3,500
  • Result: 95% occupancy rate, but 200,000 people on waiting lists
  • Deadweight loss: Mutually beneficial trades can't happen (people willing to pay $3k can't find apartments)
  • Resources misallocated: Some rent-controlled residents have extra bedrooms, others desperately need space

Uber Surge Pricing (Efficiency in Action):

  • New Year's Eve 2023: Normal ride ¬£15, surge to ¬£45 (3x multiplier)
  • Demand side: Only those who really value ride (getting home safely) pay ¬£45
  • Supply side: Drivers cancel plans to work for ¬£45/ride
  • Result: Market clears - everyone willing to pay ¬£45 gets ride, no shortages
  • Allocatively efficient: Rides go to those who value them most

The Debate: Surge pricing is economically efficient but feels "unfair." Rent control feels compassionate but creates deadweight loss and shortages. This illustrates the equity vs efficiency trade-off in economics.

Source: NYC Rent Guidelines Board 2023 Report; Uber Economic Impact Study 2024

Core Concept

Markets are classified into four main structures based on number of firms, product differentiation, entry barriers, and pricing power. Understanding these helps analyze competitive dynamics and predict firm behavior.

Structure Firms Products Entry Example

Perfect Competition: The Five Characteristics

Core Concept

Perfect Competition is a theoretical market structure where ALL five conditions must be met simultaneously. It serves as a benchmark for evaluating real markets and understanding competitive dynamics.

The 5 Essential Conditions

  1. Many Buyers & Sellers: So numerous that no single participant can influence market price. Each firm's output is negligible compared to total market supply.
  2. Homogeneous (Identical) Products: Perfect substitutes - consumers cannot distinguish between suppliers. No branding, quality differences, or product differentiation.
  3. Perfect Information: All participants have complete knowledge of prices, quality, and production methods across all suppliers. No information asymmetry exists.
  4. Free Entry & Exit: No barriers prevent new firms entering or existing firms leaving. No licensing, patents, high capital requirements, or sunk costs.
  5. Price Takers: Firms must accept market price determined by supply and demand. If one firm charges above market price, it loses ALL customers instantly.

Key Implication: In perfect competition, P = MR = AR. The firm faces a perfectly elastic (horizontal) demand curve at market price.

üí∞ Real-World Example: Bitcoin Mining (2020-2023)

Cryptocurrency mining (especially Bitcoin) surprisingly approximates some perfect competition characteristics:

Characteristics Present:

  • Many sellers: 1 million+ Bitcoin miners globally compete to validate blocks
  • Homogeneous product: One Bitcoin is identical to any other Bitcoin (fungible)
  • Perfect information: Bitcoin price public 24/7 on exchanges, blockchain is transparent
  • Price takers: Individual miner cannot influence Bitcoin price ($43,000 on Feb 8, 2024)
  • Relatively free entry: Anyone can buy mining equipment and compete

The Competitive Reality:

  • Mining profitability in Feb 2024: ~$0.08 per kWh of electricity used
  • Miners with electricity costs >$0.08/kWh shut down (can't compete)
  • When Bitcoin price rose from $16K (Nov 2022) to $43K (Feb 2024), new miners entered
  • Increased competition ‚Üí mining difficulty increased 85% ‚Üí profit margins compressed

Why it's NOT perfectly competitive: High capital costs (ASIC miners cost $3,000-12,000), electricity access varies by region, and economies of scale favor large operations. But it's closer than most industries!

Source: Blockchain.com Mining Statistics; Cambridge Bitcoin Electricity Consumption Index, Feb 2024

The Perfectly Competitive Firm as Price Taker

Core Concept

In perfect competition, individual firms are price takers - they must accept the market price determined by industry supply and demand. The firm faces a perfectly elastic demand curve (horizontal line) at the market price.

P = MR = AR (at all output levels)

In Plain English

Imagine you're a wheat farmer. The Chicago Board of Trade shows wheat at $6.50/bushel. That's THE price. You can't charge $7.00 (customers buy from another farmer instantly) or $6.00 (why would you take less when market pays $6.50?).

You're a "price taker" - you take the market price or don't sell.

Key Insights:

  • Can sell any quantity at market price (demand perfectly elastic)
  • Total Revenue = Price √ó Quantity
  • Marginal Revenue = Price (each additional unit sells for market price)
  • Firm's only decision: HOW MUCH to produce

üåæ Real-World Example: Wheat Farmer in Kansas (2024)

Scenario: John's farm produces wheat in competitive global market.

Market Price (CBOT): $6.20/bushel

  • John's farm output: 50,000 bushels/year
  • Total Kansas output: 240 million bushels/year
  • John's market share: 0.00002% (negligible!)

John's Pricing "Power" (None):

  • If John charges $6.50: Zero sales (buyers go to other farmers at $6.20)
  • If John charges $6.00: Sells out immediately but loses $0.20 √ó 50,000 = $10,000
  • If John charges $6.20: Sells all 50,000 bushels

John's Revenue Calculation:

  • Total Revenue = $6.20 √ó 50,000 = $310,000
  • Marginal Revenue (per bushel) = $6.20 (constant)
  • Average Revenue (per bushel) = $6.20 (constant)

John's ONLY decision: Should he plant 50,000 bushels, or 60,000, or 40,000? He chooses output, not price.

Source: USDA National Agricultural Statistics Service; CBOT Wheat Futures Data, Feb 2024

Short-Run Profit Maximization

Core Concept

Firms maximize profit by producing where MR = MC (Marginal Revenue equals Marginal Cost). In perfect competition, since MR = P, firms produce where P = MC.

Profit Maximization Rule: MR = MC (or P = MC in perfect competition)

In Plain English

The logic is simple:

  • If producing one more unit brings in MORE revenue than it costs (MR > MC) ‚Üí make it!
  • If producing one more unit costs MORE than it brings in (MR < MC) ‚Üí don't make it!
  • Sweet spot: produce until MR = MC (can't improve profit further)

Example: Coffee costs ¬£2 to make, sells for ¬£4. MR (¬£4) > MC (¬£2) ‚Üí keep producing! But the 100th coffee costs ¬£4.50 to make due to overtime wages. MR (¬£4) < MC (¬£4.50) ‚Üí stop at 99 coffees!

üöó Real-World Example: Uber Driver Shift Decision (2024)

Scenario: Maria drives for Uber in London on Friday night.

Market Price (fare after Uber's cut): ¬£8/ride

  • Rides 1-5: Marginal cost = ¬£3/ride (petrol, wear)
  • Rides 6-10: MC = ¬£5/ride (getting tired, further from home)
  • Rides 11-15: MC = ¬£7/ride (very tired, late night)
  • Ride 16+: MC = ¬£10/ride (exhausted, far from home, next day work concerns)

Profit Maximization Decision:

  • Rides 1-15: MR (¬£8) > MC (¬£3-7) ‚Üí Do these rides!
  • Ride 16: MR (¬£8) < MC (¬£10) ‚Üí STOP HERE
  • Optimal output: 15 rides where MR = MC (approximately)

What if surge pricing hits 2x (¬£16/ride)?

  • Now MR = ¬£16
  • Rides 16-20 now have MC < ¬£16
  • Maria works longer, produces more rides!
  • New equilibrium: ~22 rides where MC rises to ~¬£16

This demonstrates how firms respond to price changes by adjusting output to where P = MC.

Source: Uber Driver Economics Study, London 2024; Rideshare Guy Survey Data

Short-Run Outcomes: Profit, Loss, or Shutdown?

Core Concept

In the short run, perfectly competitive firms can experience four outcomes depending on where price falls relative to costs:

  • Supernormal Profit: P > ATC (price above average total cost)
  • Normal Profit (Break-even): P = ATC (covering all costs including opportunity cost)
  • Loss but Continue: AVC < P < ATC (covering variable costs, minimizing loss)
  • Shutdown: P < AVC (can't even cover variable costs)

In Plain English

Shutdown Rule: If revenue can't cover variable costs (wages, materials, utilities), shut down immediately. You're losing LESS money by closing than staying open!

Continue Operating at Loss Rule: If you're covering variable costs but not fixed costs (rent, loan payments), keep operating in short run. Fixed costs are sunk - you owe them anyway. At least minimize your loss.

üè† Real-World Example: Airbnb Hosts During COVID-19 (2020)

Pre-COVID (Supernormal Profit):

  • Average nightly rate: ¬£120
  • Variable costs per night: ¬£20 (cleaning, utilities)
  • Fixed costs per month: ¬£1,200 (mortgage)
  • Occupancy: 20 nights/month
  • Monthly profit: (¬£120-20)√ó20 - ¬£1,200 = ¬£800 supernormal profit!

March-August 2020 (Operating at Loss):

  • Travel collapsed, rates fell to ¬£60/night
  • Occupancy: 8 nights/month
  • Variable cost: Still ¬£20/night
  • Revenue: ¬£60√ó8 = ¬£480
  • Variable costs: ¬£20√ó8 = ¬£160
  • Contribution to fixed costs: ¬£320
  • Fixed costs: ¬£1,200
  • Loss: -¬£880/month

Why Keep Operating?

  • If shutdown: Lose ¬£1,200 (still owe mortgage)
  • If continue: Lose ¬£880 (at least covering ¬£320 of mortgage)
  • Better to lose ¬£880 than ¬£1,200!

When to Shutdown (Some hosts did):

  • If rates fell below ¬£20 (couldn't cover cleaning/utilities)
  • Then revenue < variable cost ‚Üí shutdown immediately
  • Many hosts mothballed properties May-July 2020
Source: AirDNA Market Reports 2020; Airbnb Host Economics Survey, COVID Impact Study

Long-Run Equilibrium: Zero Economic Profit

Core Concept

In the long run, perfect competition drives economic profit to zero. Firms earn only normal profit (covering all costs including opportunity cost). This occurs through the entry/exit mechanism.

Long-Run Equilibrium: P = MR = MC = ATC (minimum)

The Entry/Exit Mechanism

If supernormal profit exists:

  • New firms attracted by profit ‚Üí Enter market
  • Market supply increases (shifts right)
  • Market price falls
  • Profit squeezed until P = ATC (zero economic profit)

If losses exist:

  • Firms exit market to avoid losses
  • Market supply decreases (shifts left)
  • Market price rises
  • Losses eliminated until P = ATC

Result: Long-run equilibrium where remaining firms earn normal profit, produce at minimum ATC (most efficient scale).

üçï Real-World Example: Pizza Restaurant Industry in London (2020-2024)

2020 Lockdown (Supernormal Profit):

  • Delivery-only model during lockdowns
  • Domino's, Papa John's: 40% profit margins (unusually high)
  • Average pizza price: ¬£18, cost: ¬£11, profit: ¬£7

2021-2022 (Entry Wave):

  • Seeing high profits, new pizza places opened everywhere
  • London: 2,400 pizza restaurants (2020) ‚Üí 3,800 (2022) = 58% increase!
  • Cloud kitchens, independents, chains all entering
  • Low barriers: Rent kitchen, get Deliveroo/Uber Eats account, start selling

2023 (Supply Increase, Price War):

  • Massive competition ‚Üí price cutting to attract customers
  • Average price fell: ¬£18 ‚Üí ¬£14
  • Profit margins compressed: ¬£7 ‚Üí ¬£2 per pizza

2024 (Exit Wave, Equilibrium Approaching):

  • 400+ pizza restaurants closed in London (2023-24)
  • Only surviving: Most efficient operators at minimum ATC
  • Profit margins: ~¬£2-3 (normal profit, covering opportunity cost)
  • Market stabilizing with fewer, more efficient firms

This demonstrates the entry/exit mechanism driving toward zero economic profit in competitive markets!

Economic Efficiency: Why Perfect Competition is "Ideal"

Core Concept

Perfect competition achieves TWO types of economic efficiency that maximize social welfare:

  • Allocative Efficiency: P = MC (society's optimal resource allocation)
  • Productive Efficiency: Production at minimum ATC (no waste)

Understanding the Efficiency Types

Allocative Efficiency (P = MC):

  • Last unit produced: value to society (P) = cost to produce (MC)
  • Resources allocated exactly where society values them most
  • No deadweight loss - total surplus maximized

Productive Efficiency (Minimum ATC):

  • Firms produce at lowest possible average cost
  • No waste - using optimal combination of inputs
  • Operating at most efficient scale

Why Monopolies Fail Both: Monopolies produce where P > MC (allocative inefficiency) and rarely at minimum ATC (productive inefficiency).

üí± Real-World Example: Foreign Exchange Markets vs Pharmaceutical Monopoly

FX Markets (Near-Perfect Competition = Efficient):

  • Converting USD to EUR: Spread is 0.0001% (virtually zero markup)
  • Price = Marginal cost (allocatively efficient)
  • Execution in milliseconds at minimal cost (productively efficient)
  • $7.5 trillion daily volume, millions of participants
  • Benefits: Travelers, businesses get fair exchange rates

Pharmaceutical Patents (Monopoly = Inefficient):

  • Humira (arthritis drug) under patent 2003-2023
  • Price: $7,200/month in US
  • Marginal cost to produce: ~$100/month
  • P >> MC = Allocative inefficiency (deadweight loss)
  • Many patients who'd benefit (value > $100) but can't afford $7,200 don't get treatment

What Happened When Patent Expired (2023)?

  • Generic biosimilars entered (competitive market)
  • Price fell to $1,200-2,000/month (approaching MC)
  • More patients gained access
  • Moving toward allocative efficiency

This demonstrates why economists favor competition - it drives efficiency and maximizes social welfare.

Source: Bank for International Settlements Triennial Survey 2022; AbbVie Annual Reports; FDA Biosimilar Approvals 2023

Limitations of Perfect Competition Model

Core Concept

Perfect competition is a theoretical benchmark, not a realistic description of most markets. Understanding its limitations is crucial for real-world policy analysis.

Why Perfect Competition Rarely Exists

  • 1. Product Differentiation is Everywhere: Branding, quality differences, location advantages mean products are rarely identical
  • 2. Information is Never Perfect: Consumers don't know all prices, sellers don't know all costs, asymmetric information common
  • 3. Economies of Scale Create Barriers: Large firms produce cheaper than small firms, preventing "many small firms" assumption
  • 4. Innovation Requires Profit: Zero economic profit eliminates incentive for R&D investment
  • 5. Barriers Exist Everywhere: Licensing, patents, sunk costs, brand loyalty prevent free entry

üíä Real-World Example: Why Pharmaceutical Industry CAN'T Be Perfectly Competitive

The Innovation Problem:

  • Developing new drug: $2.6 billion cost, 10-15 years
  • 90% of drugs in trials fail
  • If perfect competition existed: P = MC immediately
  • Result: Zero profit ‚Üí No funding for R&D ‚Üí No new drugs!

Case Study: COVID-19 Vaccines (2020-2021):

  • Pfizer invested $2B in mRNA vaccine development (risky!)
  • Patent protection allowed pricing at $20/dose (MC ~$1.50)
  • Supernormal profit: $37B revenue in 2021
  • Why society accepts this monopoly: Without profit incentive, no vaccine would exist!

The Trade-Off:

  • Perfect competition = Allocative efficiency TODAY but no innovation TOMORROW
  • Monopoly/patents = Inefficiency TODAY but innovation incentive for FUTURE
  • Society chooses: 20-year patents (temporary monopoly) to reward innovation

After Patent Expires: Generic manufacturers enter, price falls toward MC, market becomes competitive. Best of both worlds: Innovation incentive + eventual competition!

Source: Tufts Center for Drug Development 2020; Pfizer Annual Report 2021; FDA Patent Database
